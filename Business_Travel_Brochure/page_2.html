COLLECTIVE VALUES AND COMMUNICATION

Japan also has an extensive subculture, some of which have had their interest wane. Some of Japan's subcultures are the "Kogal", "Itasha" and "Cosplay" cultures. Itasha is a fad whereby those involved decorate their cars with characters from manga, anime or video games. Itasha had its start in the 1980s but didn't gain much of a following until the 21st century (Itasha, 2012, par. 1-3). Cosplay is a role-playing performance where the participants dress in costume with accessories to depict an idea or a character. Popular sources of reference include anime, manga, video games and comics but anything that can be understood theatrically may be used. It is also given a generalized definition to include any form of costumed role-play aside from the stage performances (Cosplay, 2012, par. 1-2). Kogal is a female fashion trend that requires wearing a Japanese school uniform with a shortened skirt and loosened socks. Often a Kogal will have her hair dyed and her skin tanned. The fad was popular in the 90's but has since seen its popularity drop (Kogal, 2012, par. 1-2).

The Japanese use verbal and non-verbal communication to convey a message. Non-verbal types of communications include facial expressions, posture and the tone of one's voice. Expressions such as frowning would be interpreted as disagreement. It is also considered disrespectful to stare into someone else's eyes (Kwintessential, n.d, "Japanese Non-Verbal Communication"). The eDiplomat website says "Nodding is very important. When listening to Japanese speak...you should nod to show you are listening and understanding the speaker." The Japanese also have a concept called mentsu or "face" and the Kwintessential website states "Saving face is crucial in Japanese society" and "that turning down someone's request causes embarrassment and loss of face to the other person". On the contrary, doing something to build up a person around others can give face (Rochelle K., 2010, par. 2).
